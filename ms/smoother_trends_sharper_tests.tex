\begin{abstract}
Event study designs estimate a sequence of treatment effects $\hat{\beta}_t$ from two-way fixed effects regressions. These estimates are noisy, with known standard errors that vary across periods. We propose treating the $\hat{\beta}_t$ sequence as observations from a local linear trend state-space model and applying the Rauch--Tung--Striebel (Kalman) smoother to recover the underlying treatment effect trajectory $\beta_t$ and its derivative $\Delta\beta_t$. The approach exploits the known heteroskedastic observation noise (the regression standard errors) and provides joint optimal estimates of the treatment effect level and its rate of change. In Monte Carlo simulations, the Kalman smoother reduces level MSE by 62--82\% and derivative MSE by 77--98\% relative to raw estimates. A bootstrap-calibrated derivative-based parallel trends test achieves correct size and 6--7$\times$ the power of the standard Wald test for detecting pre-trend violations. We discuss assumptions, the role of the process noise specification, and limitations including the smoothness prior's interaction with sharp treatment onset.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Event study designs are among the most widely used tools in applied economics. The standard implementation estimates period-specific treatment effects $\hat{\beta}_t$ for periods around a treatment date, plots them with confidence intervals, and assesses whether the pre-treatment coefficients are jointly zero (the parallel trends test). This approach has well-known limitations. The period-specific estimates are noisy, their first differences are noisier still, and the standard joint test for parallel trends has limited power against economically meaningful alternatives \citep[see][for recent discussions]{roth2022pretest, rambachan2023more}.

We observe that the sequence $\{\hat{\beta}_t\}$ is, by construction, a noisy time series with known, heteroskedastic observation noise: each $\hat{\beta}_t$ is normally distributed around the true $\beta_t$ with variance $\hat{\sigma}^2_t$ known from the regression. The true treatment effect trajectory $\beta_t$ is a smooth function of time---it represents the causal impact of a policy that evolves according to economic dynamics. This is precisely the setting for which the Kalman filter and smoother were designed: recovering a smooth latent state from noisy observations with known measurement error variances.

Our contribution is to formalize this connection, implement the Rauch--Tung--Striebel (RTS) smoother for event study coefficient sequences, and demonstrate its properties. The approach delivers three benefits: (i) substantially more efficient estimates of the treatment effect level; (ii) direct, jointly optimal estimates of the treatment effect \emph{derivative} (whether the effect is growing, stable, or fading); and (iii) a more powerful test for parallel trends violations, properly calibrated via parametric bootstrap.

\subsection{Related Work}

The parallel trends assumption and its testable implications have been extensively studied. \citet{roth2022pretest} shows that pre-testing for parallel trends conditions the estimated treatment effect on having passed a low-power test, inducing bias. \citet{rambachan2023more} propose sensitivity analyses that relax parallel trends to allow for bounded violations. \citet{freyaldenhoven2019pre} develop methods to detect and correct for pre-trends using instrumental variables. Our approach is complementary: rather than relaxing the assumption, we propose a more powerful test for detecting its violation.

State-space models and the Kalman filter have a long history in time series econometrics \citep{harvey1989forecasting, durbin2012time}. \citet{harvey1985trends} showed that the Hodrick--Prescott filter is a special case of the Kalman smoother for a local linear trend model. Our contribution applies this insight to the specific context of event study coefficients, where the observation noise is known from the regression rather than estimated.

Smoothing event study estimates is not new per se---researchers routinely apply local polynomial or LOESS smoothers to the $\hat{\beta}_t$ path in figures. The Kalman smoother improves on these in three respects: it uses the known heteroskedastic standard errors to weight observations optimally; it jointly estimates level and derivative; and it provides posterior uncertainty quantification for both.

\section{Setup and Notation}

Consider a canonical event study with $T_{\rm pre}$ pre-treatment and $T_{\rm post}$ post-treatment periods, for a total of $T = T_{\rm pre} + T_{\rm post}$ periods indexed by $t = 1, \ldots, T$. Treatment occurs at period $t_0 = T_{\rm pre} + 1$.

From a two-way fixed effects regression on a panel of $N$ units, we obtain period-specific coefficient estimates $\hat{\beta}_t$ and their standard errors $\hat{\sigma}_t$ for each period $t$ (with one period normalized to zero for identification). We assume the standard asymptotic approximation holds:

\begin{assumption}[Normality of Estimates]\label{a:normal}
$\hat{\beta}_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\beta_t, \sigma^2_t)$ independently across $t$, where $\beta_t$ is the true treatment effect at period $t$ and $\sigma^2_t$ is consistently estimated by $\hat{\sigma}^2_t$.
\end{assumption}

The independence across $t$ follows from the TWFE regression structure when clusters are independent across time or when the panel is sufficiently long. We discuss violations of this assumption in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\begin{assumption}[Known Observation Noise]\label{a:known_se}
The standard errors $\hat{\sigma}_t$ are treated as known. In practice they are estimated, but for moderate $N$ the estimation error in $\hat{\sigma}_t$ is second-order relative to the sampling error in $\hat{\beta}_t$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Smooth Treatment Effect Trajectory]\label{a:smooth}
The true treatment effect $\beta_t$ is well-approximated locally by a linear trend: $\beta_{t+1} \approx \beta_t + \Delta\beta_t$, where $\Delta\beta_t$ (the period-to-period change) varies slowly.
\end{assumption}

Assumption~\ref{a:smooth} is the substantive modeling choice. It encodes the prior that treatment effects evolve according to economic dynamics---adoption, behavioral adjustment, institutional change---which are inherently smooth processes. It is violated when treatment effects exhibit sharp discontinuities between adjacent periods. We quantify the consequences of this violation in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity}.

\section{The Kalman Smoother for Event Studies}

\subsection{State-Space Formulation}

We model the treatment effect as a local linear trend:
\begin{align}
\text{State:} \quad \mathbf{x}_t &= \begin{pmatrix} \beta_t \\ \Delta\beta_t \end{pmatrix}, \quad
\mathbf{x}_{t+1} = \mathbf{F} \mathbf{x}_t + \mathbf{w}_t, \quad \mathbf{w}_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{Q}) \label{eq:transition} \\
\text{Observation:} \quad \hat{\beta}_t &= \mathbf{H} \mathbf{x}_t + v_t, \quad v_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_t) \label{eq:obs}
\end{align}
where
\[
\mathbf{F} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
\mathbf{Q} = \begin{pmatrix} q_\ell & 0 \\ 0 & q_s \end{pmatrix}.
\]

The transition equation (\ref{eq:transition}) says that the level $\beta_t$ evolves by adding its current slope $\Delta\beta_t$, and the slope itself follows a random walk. The process noise $\mathbf{Q}$ governs how rapidly the trajectory can deviate from a linear trend: $q_\ell$ controls level perturbations and $q_s$ controls slope changes.

The observation equation (\ref{eq:obs}) is the event study regression output: $\hat{\beta}_t$ is a noisy observation of the true level $\beta_t$, with noise variance $\sigma^2_t$ known from the regression. This is the key structural advantage over generic time series settings: the observation noise is directly available from the econometric model rather than requiring estimation.

\subsection{Rauch--Tung--Striebel Smoother}

The Kalman filter provides the \emph{filtered} estimate $\mathbf{x}_{t|t} = \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}_t \mid \hat{\beta}_1, \ldots, \hat{\beta}_t]$, using only past and current observations. For retrospective event study analysis, where all periods are observed, we use the RTS smoother to obtain the \emph{smoothed} estimate $\mathbf{x}_{t|T} = \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}_t \mid \hat{\beta}_1, \ldots, \hat{\beta}_T]$, which uses the entire sequence.

\paragraph{Forward pass (Kalman filter).} For $t = 1, \ldots, T$:
\begin{align}
\text{Predict:} \quad \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t-1} &= \mathbf{F}\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t-1|t-1}, \quad
\mathbf{P}_{t|t-1} = \mathbf{F}\mathbf{P}_{t-1|t-1}\mathbf{F}^\top + \mathbf{Q} \\
\text{Update:} \quad \mathbf{K}_t &= \mathbf{P}_{t|t-1}\mathbf{H}^\top(\mathbf{H}\mathbf{P}_{t|t-1}\mathbf{H}^\top + \sigma^2_t)^{-1} \\
\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t} &= \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t-1} + \mathbf{K}_t(\hat{\beta}_t - \mathbf{H}\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t-1}) \\
\mathbf{P}_{t|t} &= (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_t\mathbf{H})\mathbf{P}_{t|t-1}
\end{align}

The Kalman gain $\mathbf{K}_t$ adapts to the local signal-to-noise ratio. When $\sigma^2_t$ is large (an imprecisely estimated coefficient), $\mathbf{K}_t$ shrinks and the smoother relies on the trend model. When $\sigma^2_t$ is small (a precise estimate), $\mathbf{K}_t$ is large and the smoother tracks the data. This adaptive weighting is the core advantage over fixed-window smoothers such as LOESS or Savitzky--Golay filters.

\paragraph{Backward pass (RTS smoother).} For $t = T-1, \ldots, 1$:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{C}_t &= \mathbf{P}_{t|t}\mathbf{F}^\top\mathbf{P}_{t+1|t}^{-1} \\
\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|T} &= \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t} + \mathbf{C}_t(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t+1|T} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t+1|t}) \\
\mathbf{P}_{t|T} &= \mathbf{P}_{t|t} + \mathbf{C}_t(\mathbf{P}_{t+1|T} - \mathbf{P}_{t+1|t})\mathbf{C}_t^\top
\end{align}

The smoothed state $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|T}$ provides: (i) $\hat{\beta}^{\rm KS}_t = [\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|T}]_1$, the smoothed treatment effect, with posterior variance $[\mathbf{P}_{t|T}]_{11}$; and (ii) $\widehat{\Delta\beta}^{\rm KS}_t = [\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|T}]_2$, the smoothed derivative of the treatment effect, with posterior variance $[\mathbf{P}_{t|T}]_{22}$.

\begin{proposition}[Optimality]\label{prop:optimal}
Under Assumptions~\ref{a:normal}--\ref{a:smooth}, the RTS smoother provides the minimum mean squared error linear estimate of $\beta_t$ and $\Delta\beta_t$ given $\{\hat{\beta}_1, \ldots, \hat{\beta}_T\}$.
\end{proposition}
This follows directly from the optimality of the Kalman smoother for linear-Gaussian state-space models \citep{durbin2012time}.

\section{Inference}\label{sec:inference}

\subsection{Bootstrap-Calibrated Parallel Trends Tests}

The Kalman smoother introduces serial dependence in the smoothed estimates, which invalidates the chi-squared reference distribution for Wald-type tests. We propose a parametric bootstrap to obtain correctly calibrated critical values.

\paragraph{Wald test on smoothed coefficients.} Define
\begin{equation}
W_{\rm KS} = \sum_{t=1}^{T_{\rm pre}} \left(\frac{\hat{\beta}^{\rm KS}_t}{\sqrt{[\mathbf{P}_{t|T}]_{11}}}\right)^2.
\end{equation}
Under $H_0: \beta_t = 0$ for all $t \le T_{\rm pre}$, the distribution of $W_{\rm KS}$ is not chi-squared because the smoothed estimates are correlated. However, its null distribution depends only on the known quantities $\{\sigma_t\}_{t=1}^T$ and the process noise $\mathbf{Q}$, allowing exact simulation.

\paragraph{Derivative-based test.} We propose a new test based on the smoothed slopes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:deriv_test}
D_{\rm KS} = \sum_{t=1}^{T_{\rm pre}} \left(\frac{\widehat{\Delta\beta}^{\rm KS}_t}{\sqrt{[\mathbf{P}_{t|T}]_{22}}}\right)^2.
\end{equation}
Under $H_0$, both $\beta_t = 0$ and $\Delta\beta_t = 0$ for $t \le T_{\rm pre}$. The derivative test is sensitive to violations where the treatment effect is trending toward (or away from) zero before treatment---precisely the anticipation effects that are most concerning for identification.

\paragraph{Parametric bootstrap procedure.} To obtain the critical value $c_\alpha$ for either test at level $\alpha$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item For $b = 1, \ldots, B$:
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Draw $\hat{\beta}^{(b)}_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \hat{\sigma}^2_t)$ for $t = 1, \ldots, T$ (simulate under $H_0$).
  \item Apply the Kalman smoother to $\{\hat{\beta}^{(b)}_t\}$ with the same $\mathbf{Q}$ and $\{\hat{\sigma}_t\}$.
  \item Compute the test statistic $S^{(b)}$ (either $W_{\rm KS}$ or $D_{\rm KS}$).
  \end{enumerate}
\item Set $c_\alpha$ to the $(1-\alpha)$ quantile of $\{S^{(1)}, \ldots, S^{(B)}\}$.
\item Reject $H_0$ if the observed statistic exceeds $c_\alpha$.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{proposition}[Size Control]\label{prop:size}
The bootstrap-calibrated test has rejection probability $\alpha + O(B^{-1/2})$ under $H_0$ and Assumptions~\ref{a:normal}--\ref{a:known_se}. The $O(B^{-1/2})$ term is simulation error from the bootstrap, controllable by increasing $B$.
\end{proposition}

The proof follows from the fact that the parametric bootstrap generates draws from the exact null distribution of the test statistic (since $\hat{\beta}_t$ is Gaussian with known variance under the null), so the bootstrap quantile consistently estimates the true quantile.

\section{Monte Carlo Evidence}\label{sec:mc}

\subsection{Design}

We consider a panel with $T_{\rm pre} = T_{\rm post} = 12$, yielding 24 periods. Treatment effects follow five patterns: (i) \emph{null}: $\beta_t = 0$ for all $t$; (ii) \emph{gradual}: $\beta_t = 0.5(1 - e^{-0.3(t-t_0+1)})$ for $t \ge t_0$; (iii) \emph{immediate}: $\beta_t = 0.4$ for $t \ge t_0$; (iv) \emph{fadeout}: $\beta_t = 0.5 e^{-0.1(t-t_0)}$ for $t \ge t_0$; (v) \emph{anticipation}: small pre-trend starting 3 periods before treatment.

For each pattern, we simulate $\hat{\beta}_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\beta_t, \sigma^2_t)$ with heteroskedastic $\sigma_t$ (inflated in early and late periods). We consider three configurations: $(N, \sigma) \in \{(200, 1.0), (100, 1.0), (200, 2.0)\}$. The Kalman smoother uses $q_\ell = 0.002$, $q_s = 0.001$. Results are based on 1{,}000 simulations; bootstrap critical values use $B = 499$.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{When the approach works best.} The Kalman smoother is most beneficial when: (i) the treatment effect evolves smoothly; (ii) the period-specific estimates have large standard errors (small samples, noisy outcomes); and (iii) standard errors vary across periods (heteroskedastic noise, where adaptive weighting matters most). It is least beneficial for sharp, immediate treatment effects with precisely estimated coefficients.

\paragraph{The smoothness prior and sharp effects.} For the ``immediate'' pattern (a discrete jump at $t_0$), the Kalman smoother still reduces MSE by 70\% for levels and 86\% for derivatives. However, the smoother will attenuate the estimated jump at $t_0$ and propagate some of the post-treatment signal backward into pre-treatment periods. This is visible in the simulation: the smoothed pre-treatment estimates are slightly nonzero even under the immediate DGP. The backward propagation is a feature of the RTS smoother (it uses future observations), and it is correctly reflected in the wider posterior uncertainty bands in those periods. The bootstrap-calibrated tests account for this propagation and maintain correct size.

\paragraph{Independence across periods.} Assumption~\ref{a:normal} requires independence of $\hat{\beta}_t$ across $t$. In practice, estimated event study coefficients exhibit some serial correlation, particularly in designs with staggered treatment timing or serial correlation in the error term. Extensions to the state-space model that incorporate correlated observation noise (by modeling $v_t$ as an AR(1) process, for example) are straightforward but require estimation of the autocorrelation parameter.

\paragraph{Normalization.} Standard event study designs normalize one coefficient (typically $\beta_{t_0-1}$) to zero. The Kalman smoother can accommodate this either by treating the normalized period as a very precise observation at zero (setting $\sigma_{t_0-1}$ to a small value) or by applying the smoother to the unnormalized coefficients and adjusting afterward.

\paragraph{Comparison with \citet{rambachan2023more}.} Our approach and the Rambachan--Roth sensitivity analysis address different aspects of the parallel trends problem. Rambachan--Roth asks: ``Given that we cannot precisely test parallel trends, how sensitive are the conclusions to bounded violations?'' We ask: ``Can we test parallel trends more powerfully by optimally smoothing the evidence?'' The approaches are complementary. Our Kalman-smoothed estimates could serve as inputs to the Rambachan--Roth sensitivity framework, potentially yielding tighter bounds by reducing the noise in the estimated pre-trend.

\paragraph{Choice of $\mathbf{Q}$.} The process noise matrix $\mathbf{Q}$ is a tuning parameter that encodes the researcher's prior on the smoothness of the treatment effect. This is analogous to bandwidth choice in nonparametric regression. One could select $\mathbf{Q}$ by cross-validation (leaving out one period at a time), by marginal likelihood maximization, or by calibration to the observed variability of the $\hat{\beta}_t$. We recommend a principled default but acknowledge that the results depend on this choice and urge researchers to report sensitivity analyses across a range of $\mathbf{Q}$ values.

\section{Conclusion}

Event study coefficient sequences are noisy observations of a smooth latent process with known, heteroskedastic measurement error. The Kalman smoother is the optimal linear estimator for exactly this setting. We demonstrate large efficiency gains in estimating both the treatment effect trajectory and its derivative, and propose a substantially more powerful bootstrap-calibrated test for parallel trends violations. The derivative-based test is particularly well-suited for detecting the anticipation effects and pre-trends that are most threatening to event study identification.

The approach requires the researcher to specify a smoothness prior (the process noise $\mathbf{Q}$), which should be treated as a tuning parameter with the same status as bandwidth in nonparametric regression. We provide practical guidance on its selection and encourage sensitivity analysis.
